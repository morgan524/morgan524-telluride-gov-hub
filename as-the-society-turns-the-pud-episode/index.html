As the Society Turns (the PUD Episode)

The Society Turn Development

Of all the opposition to Measure 300, none has been more "spirited" than that from Telluride Hospital District supporters. Their letters to the Daily Planet have appeared regularly, with a consistent message condemning Measure 300 and those who support it. Their chief complaint? That we are misleading the public about the Society Turn development. Our response? We admit to being insufficiently aware of the sheer size and scope of this development until recently.

Like most people in the region, we had not been following every twist and turn of the Society Turn Planned Unit Development (PUD) over the past several years. In fact, we hadn't heard much of anything about it since the Board of County Commissioners (BOCC) granted "preliminary" approval for it in May of 2023.

We believe that MANY town residents are unaware of the full scope of this proposal and know only that Society Turn is where the Hospital District plans to build its new facility. That lack of awareness shows that this project has been quietly moving forward (mostly) under the public radar, even for those planning geeks like us who tend to follow such actions closely.

Be forewarned: the size of this proposed commercial development is MASSIVE!

New information: The Hospital District's chair, Paul Reich, publicly stated on KOTO this week that the project's developer told him in "no uncertain terms" that the entire development "will not move forward if 300 passes." That is a powerful threat.

The District, understandably alarmed, has now become the PUD's most vocal advocate. This appears akin to civic Stockholm Syndrome, with the Hospital District entirely dependent on the developer, who threatens the District with severe consequences if Measure 300 passes. It's the best marketing tool that threats can buy.

We sympathize with the Hospital District. They've been promised land for a new home, but only if the developer's 400,000-square-foot project is fully approved. And if Measure 300 passes, the developer may yank the deal. So what is in this PUD proposal that apparently must be accepted in its entirety?

The Society Turn Development Proposal

The developer, Genesee, plans to build multiple four-story buildings that include commercial retail, office, some residential space, conference facilities, and a 125-room hotel. (The County Planning Commission once asked that the hotel be reconsidered, but it was never removed from the plans.) The overall development is projected to total about 400,000 sq ft, of which the new Med Center would comprise about 11%, 15% employee housing, and around 300,000 sq ft (74%) would be free-market commercial space.

Caveats: As the PUD property isn't in town, it wouldn't pay Telluride's higher taxes or be required to comply with its design standards. The County, not the Town, decides what the development looks like. Telluride would only supply its municipal water. And in-town businesses may suffer from the new retail and commercial competition built into the Society Turn development, as they will not pay the additional Telluride fees and taxes.

The hospital supporters proclaim that all this commercial development, including a 125-room hotel as the gateway to Telluride, is well worth it for the "free" land for the District and the potential upgrades to the wastewater plant. Additionally, they assert that supporters of 300 are naive to think the developer would not want something in return for the land, and this is the reasonable price to pay. Well, they may be right.

But others would say in the immortal words of the Talking Heads, "My God, what have we done!" Have we mentioned this project is MASSIVE?

What is the Status of Construction?

No one is building anything yet. CDOT hasn't approved a final traffic plan, the PUD documents haven't been updated since May 2023, and the County's final approval is likely in the next few months.

County Referendum?

Would San Miguel County residents (including Telluride and Mountain Village) have the right to challenge the Society Turn PUD through a countywide referendum if the BOCC approves it and residents are upset? No. San Miguel County is a "statutory county," meaning it follows only state law, where such a popular referendum is not allowed under any circumstances. So some say it is unfair that only Telluride voters would have a say in this project. That may well be true, but it's better than no resident of the area having such a voice in what happens.

Timing of Development

If Measure 300 passes, the Hospital District is free to set a special election 60 days after approval. (The District has held two successful special elections in the last two years, so that would continue the trend.) The District does not need to obtain signatures but can "refer" the matter to a vote approved solely by the Hospital District Board. The District would have to pay the special election costs of around $15,000. They could seek Town approval in January or February for the provision of water for the entire PUD development.

However, the Hospital District clearly would prefer not to have any vote on this issue and not to be in a position where it must defend the entire PUD development to get its "free" land. Although with all the work they are doing to oppose 300 and champion this whole development PUD, we imagine they must be wondering how "free" this land really is.

Summary

The developer, Genesee, has apparently threatened the Hospital District that if Measure 300 passes, it will withdraw its PUD application, depriving the Hospital District of its land and leaving it without a current Plan B. Although this threat may be a bluff to get the District to defend the developer's plans, which they are doing admirably, nobody can know for sure how real it is.

So we tip our hats to the developer for this deal. The hospital district is now the prominent advocate for the entire Society Turn PUD, a 400,000 sq ft development of mostly commercial buildings, out of fear that the developer will pull its PUD application regardless of what Telluride voters think of this project. But we know the voters of Telluride are adults who can make informed decisions on these significant issues and balance those concerns when they vote on Measure 300.
