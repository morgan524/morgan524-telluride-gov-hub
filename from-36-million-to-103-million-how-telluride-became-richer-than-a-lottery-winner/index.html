From $36 Million to $103 Million: How Telluride Became Richer Than a Lottery Winner

From $36 Million to $103 Million: How Telluride Became Richer Than a Lottery Winner Staff Sep 13, 2025 3 min read

Ah, Telluride. The cozy little mountain town with postcard views, ski-town charm, and apparently a wallet bigger than Oprah's Christmas budget. Back in 2015, the Town's budget was a humble $10 million. Fast forward to today, and poof! It's ballooned to $103.3 million in 2025. That's not growth—that's a fiscal protein shake laced with rocket fuel. That represents a 930% increase in the Town budget over the last ten years.

You might reasonably ask, "How the hell did we get here?" Well, honestly, it's not clear at all. You can check out the nicely done budget that is available online, but without reviewing all ten years of budgets, comparing all the line items, it's hard to determine.

The Rich Uncle Problem

When your town budget starts looking like a Silicon Valley IPO, the natural instinct is to start thinking big. Really big. Like: "Do we need a new dogspa-slash-yoga-studio-slash-public-art-gallery? Of course we do!"

You know that relative who wins the lottery and suddenly decides the whole family needs matching gold-plated toilets? That's Telluride right now. Only, instead of toilets, it's consultant reports and parking garages.

The mindset shift has been dramatic:

· 2015 Council: "Sorry, we can't afford that."

· 2025 Council: "We can't afford not to build around 800,000 square feet of new stuff in Town!"

Money Burns a Hole in Your Ski Pants

Nobody wants to look stingy when they're sitting on a treasure chest. If Telluride suddenly got frugal, people would start rumors that the Town Council was hiding bars of gold under the gondola. So instead, we get projects galore, complete with glossy laminated design boards that scream, "Yes, Aspen consultants made this."

And who really benefits from all this largesse? For starters, the 212 Town employees, some making close to $200,000 a year—because why should Wall Street have all the fun? That's about 10% of the Town's entire population working for the Town. Add in the Town's goal of housing 30% of its staff, and you're basically building a small village inside the village—kind of like Russian nesting dolls, but with condos.

The Credit Card

Sure, the Town could just spend the money it has—but where's the drama in that? Nope, to really bring those shiny new projects to life, Telluride has to whip out the credit card and go shopping like it's Black Friday at Costco, and nothing says "responsible governance" like racking up debt faster than a college kid with their first Visa.

And boy, do we have receipts. Here's a quick peek at the tab we've already run up—and the fresh requests for even more debt coming down the pipeline on the November Ballot.

Spoiler alert: it makes your ski pass look like a bargain.

Conclusion: Can't Stop, Won't Stop

At the end of the day, going from a $10 million to $103 million budget is like finding a Back to the Future flux capacitor button you didn't know you had. Suddenly, every idea sounds affordable, reasonable, and dare we say… necessary. So buckle up, Telluride residents. If you thought the last few years were a town-building spree, wait to see what construction will be like during the next five years, and the associated bill!!
