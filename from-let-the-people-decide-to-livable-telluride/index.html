From "Let the People Decide" to "Livable Telluride"

We hope this email finds you all well!

You have not heard from us (much) since the “people decided” on Measure 300 in November.

So, it’s time for a name that reflects where we go from here, and that name is “Livable Telluride.”

Changing the name to Livable Telluride matters because it shifts the focus from a single ballot question to the broader, ongoing work of keeping this incredible location functional, welcoming, and worth living in for the people who actually live here.

“Livable” isn’t code for freezing the county in amber, picking winners and losers, or drawing a line between “locals” and “others.” It’s an inclusive standard: a community that works for families, workers, retirees, and newcomers alike; that protects what makes Telluride special while being honest about real-world constraints like housing, water, traffic, public services, and the cost of everyday life.

In other words, “livable” is not a limit. Rather, it’s a commitment to smart, transparent decisions that balance growth with capacity and ensure Telluride remains a place where a wide range of people can participate, belong, and thrive.

A Small Dose of Humility

“Let the People Decide” was built for one purpose: getting a specific question onto the ballot. That chapter has closed (and yes, elections usually do close chapters, unless it’s 2020, which, like a civic zombie from hell, refuses to stay buried).

If the last election taught anything, it’s that communities are complicated, people disagree in good faith, and outcomes don’t always go the way you want. That’s democracy. Livable Telluride isn’t about “winning” every argument. It’s about making sure the conversation stays grounded in real facts, real tradeoffs, and real consequences.

We’re still working out exactly how this will run, and more importantly, who will do all the work (applications accepted; coffee is negotiable). But we do know this: we’ll keep sharing useful information when it matters. Which brings us to the first item of new business.

First Issue: County Commissioner District 2

One of the earliest—and most consequential—moments in the San Miguel County election cycle is coming up really fast:

Democratic Precinct Caucus & County Assembly
Saturday, March 7, 2026, 3 pm
Wilkinson Public Library

This is where the field for the June primary gets shaped, and in a county the size of San Miguel, the small number of people in the room at this stage can have an outsized impact. It’s also fun to be involved at this retail level of local politics, so we encourage everyone to attend.

The November election of interest will be for the County Commissioner seat in District 2. While everyone in the County will vote for the candidate in the primary and November election, the candidate must reside full-time within District 2 and be a currently registered Democrat to be eligible.

The Current (Only) Candidate

At present, Paul Reich is the only publicly announced candidate (that we are aware of) for County Commissioner District 2. Mr. Reich serves on the Telluride Hospital District Board and supported approval of the Society Turn PUD, which includes a new medical facility and approximately 300,000 square feet of market-rate commercial space development, including a new hotel.

During the campaign over Measure 300, Mr. Reich was a vocal supporter of the Society Turn PUD and urged the Hospital District, the Telluride School Board, and voters to oppose Measure 300.

But regardless of your views on Society Turn, or any other issue, having multiple candidates run for this important elected position is crucial for democracy in our small county. Please consider showing up and “throwing that hat in the ring” to be a San Miguel County Commissioner candidate for District 2.
