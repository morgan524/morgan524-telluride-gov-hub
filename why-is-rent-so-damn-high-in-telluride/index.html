Why is Rent So Damn High In Telluride!

The other day, a gem of an exchange popped up on Sweet Rants (our unofficial local soap opera), and boy, was it enlightening. It cut right to the heart of the Town Council’s latest batch of developments. The summary? They. Cost. Too. Much!

Step 1: Meet TABOR

Colorado has this thing called TABOR (Taxpayer’s Bill of Rights). Basically, it’s the law’s way of saying: “Hey government, you don’t get to rack up credit card debt unless you ask the people first.” However, some loopholes allow Town to get around this voting requirement.

For example, the VooDoo project cost about $27 million. The Town subsidized the project with $6 million from its piggy bank and borrowed $21 million. Add $36 million in interest over 20 years, and voilà—you’ve basically created the world’s most expensive 27 units of ski condos ever.

Step 2: Enter State Housing Law

Colorado housing law says the Town can’t rent to anyone who makes more than five times the annual rent cost. Translation: renters must cough up at least 20% of their income for rent for “affordable housing.”

But—plot twist!—if the Town uses the TABOR loophole, then rents must cover all financing costs of the project.

Step 3: AMI (Because Acronyms Make Everything Fancier)

Affordable housing projects use something called AMI (Area Median Income) to set rents. Normally, housing helps folks making 50–100% of AMI. But Telluride is a “special rural resort snow-globe town,” so they can stretch it up to 175% AMI or higher.

Step 4: The Great Empty Unit Mystery

So what happens? High-income units sit empty because nobody in Town makes that much money (turns out ski lift operators don’t pull six figures). Meanwhile, the Town scratches its head, wondering why its shiny million-dollar units are collecting dust.

Step 5: Avoiding a Vote Creates This Problem

If the Town had asked voters to approve the debt under TABOR, they could’ve used general funds to subsidize the housing and keep rents reasonable. Instead, the Town relied on loopholes, AMI gymnastics, and a prayer to the housing gods—resulting in the great Telluride riddle of “affordable housing that nobody can actually afford.”

Step 6: Bonus Conflict of Interest!

There’s a little state law out there that says commissioners can’t “acquire any interest, direct or indirect,” in Town housing projects they voted to create. And yet… one of our fine commissioners apparently not only voted to approve the million-bucks-a-unit VooDoo project repeatedly, but is now happily leasing one of those very units.
