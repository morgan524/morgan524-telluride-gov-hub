It cut right to the heart of the Town Council's latest batch of developments. The summary? They. Cost. Too. Much! Like, "Do I need to sell a kidney or start a GoFundMe just to rent a closet?" too much.

You might ask, well, how did we get here?

Step 1: Meet TABOR

Colorado has this thing called TABOR (Taxpayer's Bill of Rights). Basically, it's the law's way of saying: "Hey government, you don't get to rack up credit card debt unless you ask the people first." However, some loopholes allow Town to get around this voting requirement.

For example, the VooDoo project cost about $27 million. The Town subsidized the project with $6 million from its piggy bank and borrowed $21 million. Add $36 million in interest over 20 years, and voilà—you've basically created the world's most expensive 27 units of ski condos ever. But wait… how did the Town avoid asking voters for permission to take on $50 million more debt?

Ah, the old TABOR loophole. If the Town's general fund doesn't back up the debt, but instead is tied to only the building itself, then—abracadabra!—it magically doesn't count as "long-term debt." No voter approval required. If there were a default on the debt, the lenders could repossess the building and sell it on the open market to recoup their costs, but they could not require the Town to pay the debt.

Step 2: Enter State Housing Law

Here's where the legalese comes in. Colorado housing law says the Town can't rent to anyone who makes more than five times the annual rent cost. Translation: renters must cough up at least 20% of their income for rent for "affordable housing." The idea is that affordable housing should be provided at an "affordable" level, but not be a steal for a renter earning many times more than needed to pay rent.

But—plot twist!—if the Town uses the TABOR loophole, then rents must cover all financing costs of the project. In plain English: "If you mortgage the building, the rent you get better cover the mortgage, and you cannot use the Town's rainy-day general fund to cover any shortfall."

So now the Town has two state rules that set minimum payments for renters and minimum income for the project that must be paid back annually:

It can't charge less than 20% of the annual income of any renter or group of renters (which is why rents are based on the renters' "tier" of income. CRS § 29-4-210.

It must charge enough rent to cover the principal and interest on an annual basis for the super-duper jumbo-sized loan. CRS § 29-4-229.

Step 3: AMI (Because Acronyms Make Everything Fancier)

Affordable housing projects use something called AMI (Area Median Income) to set rents. Someone earning 100% of AMI is earning the exact median income for the area, meaning half make more and half make less.

Normally, housing helps folks making 50–100% of AMI to keep the units for those most in need. But Telluride is a "special rural resort snow-globe town," so they can stretch it up to 175% AMI or higher. (Currently, 50% of AMI in San Miguel for a single person is about $42,000 a year, and 175% AMI is $145,000 per year.)

The problem:

If the project is filed with renters at a 50% AMI level, it won't receive enough income to cover the financing of the project as a whole. Renters at 175% AMI can pay plenty—but then it's not "affordable housing." It's "affordable-for-people-with-high-paying-Town-Government-jobs."

Step 4: The Great Empty Unit Mystery

So what happens? High-income units sit empty because nobody in Town makes that much money (turns out ski lift operators don't pull six figures). Meanwhile, the Town scratches its head, wondering why its shiny million-dollar units are collecting dust and are continually hard to rent.

Step 5: Avoiding a Vote Creates This Problem

If the Town had asked voters to approve the debt under TABOR, they could've used general funds to subsidize the housing and keep rents reasonable (albeit constantly subsidized). Under this scenario, the debt would be against the Town, which would owe the money, no matter what the rents in the building were or whether the units were occupied or not.

Instead, the Town relied on loopholes, AMI gymnastics, and a prayer to the housing gods—resulting in the great Telluride riddle of "affordable housing that nobody can actually afford." These gymnastics were all to avoid a public vote on the project, and look where we are!

Step 6: Bonus Conflict of Interest!

There's a little state law out there — think of it as the "No Self-Serve Buffet" rule for Town Council members. It says commissioners can't "acquire any interest, direct or indirect," in Town housing projects they voted to create. (CRS 29-4-207, for the sticklers, and translated into English as "don't vote yourself into a sweet new condo.")

And yet… one of our fine commissioners apparently heard "don't" as "go for it!" This mystery council member not only voted to approve the million-bucks-a-unit VooDoo project repeatedly, but is now happily leasing one of those very units. We'll let the voters of Telluride put on their detective hats and play a round of Guess That Council Member!
